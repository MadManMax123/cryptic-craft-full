Catch the Metro

A metro, short for metropolitan railway, is a high-capacity rapid transit system built to move large numbers of people quickly through dense urban areas. Most metro lines run on dedicated tracks, fully separated from road traffic, often underground in tunnels or elevated above the street on viaducts, which lets trains keep a tight, predictable schedule.

The world's first underground metro opened in London in 1863, running on steam power before electrification took over. Since then, metro networks have spread to hundreds of cities worldwide, from the sprawling systems of Tokyo, New York, and Moscow to newer builds racing to keep pace with rapid urban growth. Trains typically run on a fixed rail gauge, drawing power from a third rail or overhead wire, and stations are spaced to balance speed with accessibility.
