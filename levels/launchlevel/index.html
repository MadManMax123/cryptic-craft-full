// 001 — ORDINARY HOURS
0412 HRS. The fluorescent hum of Bay-7 control room.
You have heard it so many times it has become indistinguishable from silence.
Fourteen screens. Forty-three satellites. One massive eye staring at the earth.
And you — somewhere in the middle of the org chart, close enough to the machine
to feel its heat, far enough from authority to be invisible.
BADGE: [ CHIEF SERVER TECHNICIAN ]  |  CLEARANCE: L-3 OPERATIONS
Not the scientist. Not the director. Not the face at the press conference.
You are the one who keeps the memory alive. The servers. The data. The silence between the pings.
The NISAR instrument above captures everything — millimetre shifts in soil, coastal erosion,
tectonic breathing. Sensitive enough to know the earth is restless long before we are.
You watch the commercial radar bleed across the band. A Bangalore-to-Chennai Indigo flight.
A cargo vessel rounding the cape. Standard noise. Standard night.
Or so you believe.
// 002 — THE CALL
0551 HRS. The intercom crackles.
LEAD ENGINEER — DR. VIKRAM S.
"Are you on shift?  We have a rack issue in Server Block C. Anomalous power read. Come now."
Not a request. The kind of sentence that ends when you are already moving.
You badge through three checkpoints. The air gets colder with each one.
Block C. Row 14. The engineers are clustered around a mid-rack unit like surgeons
over something that shouldn't still be breathing.
ENGINEER — PRIYA R.
"It happened during the capture window. All archived ingest from orbital pass 7-Alpha. We may have lost twelve hours of data."
DR. VIKRAM S.
"Can you isolate it? Tell me we haven't lost the pass."
You push past them. You crouch. You look.
Board C-14-9. Burn mark near the voltage regulator. Clean, directional.
Not a surge from outside. Something drew too much from the inside.
YOU
"Circuit board. Overvolted. Regulator's gone. I can have this running in forty minutes — the data is probably fine, the RAID is on a separate bus."
DR. VIKRAM S.
"Forty minutes. Fine. Fix it."
They step back. They breathe again. They trust you.
They always trust you with the parts they don't understand.
// 003 — THE BOARD
You replace it in thirty-eight minutes. Clean swap. Systems nominal.
The engineers are satisfied. They go back to their screens.
You hold the burned board in your gloved hand.
Officially, it goes into the incinerator feed — protocol for compromised hardware.
// FACILITY PROTOCOL — HARDWARE DISPOSAL DAMAGED UNITS MUST BE INCINERATED WITHIN 4 HOURS OF REMOVAL.
NO COMPONENT MAY LEAVE THE FACILITY FLOOR.
VIOLATION: IMMEDIATE SUSPENSION OF CLEARANCE.
You know this. You walk toward the incinerator room.
You pass the slot. You do not drop it in.
You pocket it instead.
It's an ordinary board. Damaged. Useless — officially.
But the burn pattern bothers you. You have seen lightning damage, surge damage, load spikes.
This looked like something was being fed.
// 004 — YOUR LAB
0830 HRS. End of your assigned shift. You do not leave.
Sub-level maintenance lab. No cameras in here — the facility never thought
anyone would have a reason to stay.
You connect the board to an isolated machine. Air-gapped from the main network.
You are methodical. You are careful. You have done this before with faulty units.
But the board is not dead.
Under a low-voltage probe, something in it wakes.
// DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT — ISOLATED TERMINAL DEVICE DETECTED: C-14-9 [COMPROMISED]
ACTIVE INTERFACES FOUND:  1
INTERFACE TYPE:  SERIAL (LOW-BAND) — UNDOCUMENTED
BAUD RATE:  9600
STATUS:  TRANSMITTING
PAYLOAD DETECTED:  1 FILE
You stare at the screen for a long time.
This board was inside a classified ingest server. It was not supposed to have
any external-facing serial interface. It was not supposed to transmit anything.
YOU  [internal]
"This was never in the spec sheet.  This was never in any spec sheet."
The file sits in the receive buffer. Awaiting instruction.
You know what you should do. Close the connection. Log an incident report.
Hand it to the security directorate. Forget the next four hours of your life.
You open the file.
// 005 — THE BAT FILE
It executes before you can think. Not malicious — nothing trips.
No payload, no exfil, no damage. Just a text editor. Opening. A single line.
// FILE: SIGNAL_OUT.BAT — OUTPUT
ECHO:   You found it. That means you were supposed to find it.

ECHO:   Don't report this. Not yet.

ECHO:   There is a door.

PAUSE
Then a second text block opens automatically. One line.
No formatting. No sender. Just characters rendered in your terminal's default font.
// DECODED OUTPUT — EMBEDDED URL
landing404.vercel.app
. . . — — — . . .
You recognise the morse. Three dots. Three dashes. Three dots.
SOS.
The oldest distress signal known to radio operators.
Someone encoded this into a circuit board. Inside a classified server.
Inside a facility whose entire purpose is to watch the surface of the earth.
Someone was watching from inside.
// 006 — THE CHOICE
You sit in the dark of your sub-level lab for a long time.
The fan of the isolated machine cycles. The board glows faintly under the probe light.
Every instinct you have trained over a decade in this facility says:
Lock down. Report. Disconnect. Walk away.
But you built these servers. You know where every wire goes.
And no wire was supposed to carry this.
YOU  [internal]
"If this was a security breach, it would have triggered something by now.
If this was an accident, there would be no URL.
This was placed here. For someone exactly like me."
You glance at the terminal. The URL still burns on the screen.
Blue-white. Patient. Like it has been waiting a long time.
Your finger hovers over the trackpad.
Outside, somewhere far above the Bay of Bengal, the satellite continues its pass.
Watching. Recording. Missing nothing.
Nothing except whatever is happening right here,
in a room no one monitors,
with a man no one remembers.
You click.
